Shadow — Proof & Credibility Inventory
1. Purpose of this document

This document defines what evidence the landing page needs to make Shadow credible. A premium landing cannot rely only on atmosphere and big claims. It must demonstrate that Shadow is a real, thoughtful system with clear product logic, real outputs, privacy awareness and founder intent.

2. Credibility strategy

Shadow should build trust through demonstration, not persuasion.

The landing should show:

what the product does
what the outputs look like
how memory works
why privacy matters
who is building it
what users get by joining early access
why the product exists beyond trend-chasing
3. Proof types needed
3.1 Product proof

Evidence that Shadow is not just a concept.

Assets needed:

Inbox Dump screen
AI Classification card
Clarifying Question card
Memory Graph fragment
Life Circle dashboard
Mode Switcher
Labs result
Weekly Reflection report
Insight card with evidence signals

Landing placement: throughout scenes, not isolated in one “features” block.

3.2 Output proof

Evidence that Shadow generates useful outputs.

Example insight:

“Your focus drops most often after unbounded mornings, not after hard tasks. The repeated pattern is unclear first action → scrolling → guilt → delayed start.”

Example clarifying question:

“What part of this feels unresolved: the task, the expectation, or the fear of showing the result?”

Example weekly summary:

“This week had high creative signal but low recovery. You made progress when tasks were visible and small, but lost momentum when decisions stayed abstract.”

Example Life Circle observation:

“Creativity is high, but rest and focus are diverging. This usually predicts unfinished loops by the end of the week.”

Requirement: every insight should feel specific and grounded in signals, not generic life advice.

3.3 Origin proof

Evidence that Shadow has a strong reason to exist.

Founder note draft:

“Shadow began from a simple problem: I could build systems for work, but my actual life still lived in fragments. Tasks were in one place, thoughts in another, emotions nowhere, goals in my head and patterns only visible after they repeated too many times. I wanted a system that could receive life as it is — messy, emotional, unfinished — and slowly turn it into memory. Shadow is that system.”

Landing placement: Manifesto, About or lower trust section.

3.4 Privacy proof

Evidence that the product respects personal data.

Core privacy position:

Shadow handles intimate life context, so privacy is not a feature hidden in the footer. It is part of the product philosophy. The user should understand what is stored, why it is stored and how they control it.

Copy direction:

“Your memory belongs to you.”

“Shadow should help you see your life, not exploit it.”

“Review, export or delete stored memory when you need to.”

Required future details:

what data is stored
whether data is used for model training
export/delete controls
retention policy
encryption/security approach
GDPR/UY compliance direction

Landing placement: before final CTA and on separate Privacy page.

3.5 Technical proof

Evidence that Shadow is not only branding.

Topics to mention carefully:

AI structuring of raw input
memory architecture
RAG-style personal context retrieval
signal classification
life analytics layer
mode-based UX
user-controlled memory
future integrations

Avoid overloading the main landing with technical jargon. Use a short “How it works” or “Built as a real memory system” section and link to a deeper page.

3.6 Design proof

Evidence that the product is premium and intentional.

Assets:

Shady character sheet
cinematic landing interactions
UI mockups
design system tokens
motion principles
scene storyboard

This is especially important for portfolio use. The project should demonstrate product thinking, UX direction, motion logic and AI architecture.

3.7 Roadmap proof

Evidence that the product has a path beyond the landing.

Roadmap phases:

Phase 1: Capture, AI structuring, Daily Sync, basic memory.

Phase 2: Life Circle, Modes, weekly reports, Shady assistant.

Phase 3: Labs, deeper personality scans, stronger pattern detection.

Phase 4: integrations, richer RAG memory, advanced analytics.

Landing placement: optional roadmap block or early access section.

4. Early access mechanics

Recommended conversion action: Join early access.

Reason: Shadow is personal and conceptually deep. Controlled onboarding is better than a generic public signup.

What user gets:

early product access
updates on development
chance to shape the product
first access to Labs
invite to try the memory system

Recommended CTA copy:

Join early access

Build your Shadow

Enter the first circle

What happens after signup:

user enters email
receives confirmation
gets short note about what Shadow is building
may answer 2–3 onboarding questions
can optionally join product updates
5. Waitlist confirmation copy

Title: You are inside the first layer.

Body: Thanks for joining early access. Shadow is being built as a personal memory system for people who need more than another task manager. You will receive updates, product previews and the first invitation when the private version opens.

Shady microcopy: I will remember this door.

6. Proof checklist before launch

Before launch, collect:

5 raw input examples
5 structured output examples
5 clarifying questions
5 insight cards
2 weekly report examples
1 Memory Graph mockup
1 Life Circle mockup
1 Labs result
1 founder note
1 privacy statement
1 early access flow
1 short demo video or animated walkthrough
7. Credibility risks

If the landing shows no real outputs, Shadow will feel like vaporware.

If privacy is vague, users will not trust the product with personal memory.

If the founder story is absent, the product may feel like an AI trend wrapper.

If UI examples are too generic, the product will look like every AI dashboard.

If Shady dominates without product proof, the project may feel like a mascot concept rather than a serious system.
